Page 321. Five lines below Equation 8.11
Delete the phrase, "or the damping factor,"

The phrase, neper frequency is preferable for naming the quantity denoted by α in this textbook. In addition to the definition of damping factor implied in this textbook, the phrase damping factor has a plurality of other definitions applying in other contexts. For three unique examples see here and here (in "Variations and hybrid methods" section) and this textbook (See page 506 of the textbook). Furthermore, Prof. De Boer has not so far found any place where the phrase damping factor is treated synonymously with neper frequency except in undergraduate circuits and linear systems textbooks or World Wide Web pages presenting similar content. This limited context is too narrow to justify introducing the phrase damping factor here.

A nearly similar term, damping ratio, (not "factor") is uniformly well defined in many contexts, including RLC circuits, but the neper frequency (α in our textbook) and the damping ratio are not the same thing. Using our textbook's notation, the damping ratio, ζ, would be defined as ζ =  α/ωo. Since most engineering students will encounter the term damping ratio in their careers, the confusingly similar name, damping factor (also known as damping attenuation factor, attenuation factor, and damping coefficient, etc.) should not be introduced in the context of RLC circuits. The name neper frequency for α is more memorable, more universal in the literature, and thus more helpful.
Also see related errata on pages 323, 361, and I-3.         (Posted 10/28/2010, updated 9/28/2011)

Pages 534–535 First paragraph in Sec. 12.10.
The second, third, fourth, and fifth sentences of Section 12.10 contain errors. Replace "The wye source connection is used for. . . lighting and appliances use single-phase power." with:

The wye connection is commonly chosen for most sources. This is due to at least three factors. First, there is a neutral connection that can be connected to earth for safety. Second the wye connection gives line voltages that are a factor of the-square-root-of-three greater than a delta connection gives. In the case of long distance power transmission, higher line voltages reduce the line currents and consequently reduce the power losses caused by heating of the conductors (I2R). Third, with appropriate voltage levels, single-phase loads can be connected to the three-phase system by connecting them between any single line and neutral. For example in North America 208 VRMS line-to-line three-phase systems are used to deliver 120 VRMS from line to neutral. Delta connected sources are rarely used because any slight imbalance between the three phases can cause problematic circulating currents. (Circulating currents are not a problem in delta-connected loads because each phase has appreciable impedance so that it may absorb electrical power.) Delta connected sources also require considerably more complicated protection (fuses, etc.) compared to wye connected sources.

*This textbook contains a systematic error.
In many cases where an expression is integrated with respect to time the same variable, t, is used as the variable of integration and as the upper limit of the integral. One variable should not be used for two different quantities. In these cases some other variable such as λ should be substituted for the variable of integration. As an example, the first instance of this error is in equation 6.5 on page 218. Some other instances of this error include:

Equations 6.5, 6.6, 6.8, 6.15, 6.20, 6.21, 6.23, 6.34, 6.35, 6.15.2, 6.15.3, 7.12, 7.34, 7.39, 8.3, 8.28, 15.27, 15.28, 15.32

Many other instances of this problem have not yet been itemized in this errata sheet. (A few instnaces of correct equations of this type also exist in this text, for example Equation 15.70 and apparently all of the remainder of Chapter 15 is correct on this point.)

Page 459. More information on Eq. (11.5).
Equation (11.5) can be further developed to show the reactive power component. (This in addition to the development of the average power component that is already shown in the textbook.) Rearranging the second term in Eq. (11.5) to deliberately itemize the angle (θv – θi), the same as in the first term, gives

p(t) = (1/2)VmIm cos(θv – θi) + (1/2)VmIm cos[2(ωt + θv) – (θv – θi)]

Now apply the trigonometric identity cos(α – β) = cos(α)cos(β) + sin(α)sin(β)
to the second term to get

p(t) = (1/2)VmIm cos(θv – θi) +
          (1/2)VmIm cos[2(ωt + θv)]cos(θv – θi) +
          (1/2)VmIm sin[2(ωt + θv)]sin(θv – θi)

Associate the middle term above with the first term and factor out constant amounts. The instantaneous power then is:

p(t) = (1/2)VmIm cos(θv – θi)[1 + cos(2ωt + θv)] +
          (1/2)VmIm sin(θv – θi)sin[2(ωt + θv)]                                     (11.5b)

The first term above in Eq. (11.5b) represents the average power. The pulsation of the average power is represented via the time-varying multiplicative term,
[1 + cos(2ωt + θv)]. Note that this term varys between 0 and 2 as time varys. It is never negative and has an average value of 1. The coefficient in front of it then must be the average power.

P = (1/2)VmIm cos(θv – θi)

The above equation is identical to Eq. (11.8) on page 460 in the text.

Turning our attention to the second term in Eq. (11.5b) notice that it varys both positive and negative as time varys. This term has an average value of zero. It represents power that is surging back and forth and doing no net work. The second term in Eq. (11.5b) is called the instantaneous reactive power. The peak magnitude of the power that surges back and forth is defined as the amount of reactive power. We use the variable Q to represent reactive power. Reactive power is given the units of "volt-amperes reactive" and is abbreviated as "VAR" or "VARs" (plural). This abbreviation is often pronounced as a word. The peak magnitude of the second term in Eq (11.5b) is

Q = (1/2)VmIm sin(θv – θi)

DEFINITION: Reactive power is the peak magnitude of power that surges equally back and forth. It has units of volt-amperes reactive (VAR).

Now the instantanious power, Eq. (11.5b), can be expressed in terms of the average power, P, and the reactive power, Q.

p(t) = P[1 + cos(2ωt + θv)] + Qsin(2(ωt + θv))                                     (11.5c)

In Section 11.4 of this textbook it is shown that

Vrms = Vm/(sqrt(2)) and that Irms = Im/(sqrt(2))

Applying these equalities gives.

P = VrmsIrms cos(θv – θi) ,      Q = VrmsIrms sin(θv – θi)

The above equations match Eq. (11.50) on page 474 of the text.

This illustrates the calculation of the average power and the reactive power for sinusoidal currents and voltages. The angle (θv – θi) recurrs many times in discussions of AC circuits. For this reason in most textbooks and magzine articles it is simply denoted as θ. This angle is given the name power factor angle.

DEFINITION: The power factor angle or more simply, the power angle is by definition θ = (θv – θi).

Using this definition, Eq. 11.50 on page 474 can be reduced to

P = VrmsIrms cos(θ) ,          Q = VrmsIrms sin(θ)

Page 471. Eq 11.38a.
This equation subtly introduces a very important issue. In the power systems industry phasors are stated with RMS magnitudes rather than peak magnitudes. There are more implications to this than first meets the eye. In particular, the phasor transform is modified so that the phasor magnitude is in RMS units. Up to this point in this textbook, all phasor magnitudes have been peak magnitudes. In electronics work, peak magnitudes are usually used, but in power systems work RMS magnitudes are usually used.

Consider this electronics-style phasor transform having peak magnitude, used for everything in this book up to this point
v(t) = 14.14cos(ωt + 25°) V   <—>   V = 14.14/25° V

Now the very same function in a power systems-style phasor transform will have an RMS magnitude. This type of phasor transform is used for everything from this point through the end of Chapter 12.
v(t) = 14.14cos(ωt + 25°) V   <—>   Vrms = 10/25° VRMS

Just like it is fair to say that 1 foot = 12 inches, it is also fair to say that
14.14/25° V = 10/25° VRMS because of the different units employed. And of course, the inverse transform of either phasor is v(t) = 14.14cos(ωt + 25°) V.

However in this textbook, and in the general literature on power systems, the "RMS" subscript on the variable Vrms and on the unit "volts RMS" is usually omitted. The reader should understand that "unless otherwise stated" the units are RMS units when the context is power systems. For example consider Equation 11.41 on page 473. The equation is S = VrmsI*rms. The very same equation appears again in Equation 11.55 on page 478, but now the subscripts are missing, S = VI*. It means exactly the same thing and the magnitudes of the voltage and current phasors are still intended to be in RMS units even though the subscripts are missing.

Note also in passing that boldface denotes a phasor (complex in general) and normal text denotes a real number and that Vrms = |Vrms|.
