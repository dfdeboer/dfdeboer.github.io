Page 321. Five lines below Equation 8.114
Delete the phrase, "or the damping factor,"

The phrase, neper frequency is preferable for naming the quantity denoted by α in this textbook. In addition to the definition of damping factor implied in this textbook, the phrase damping factor has a plurality of other definitions applying in other contexts. For three unique examples see here and here and this textbook (See page 506 of the textbook). Furthermore, Prof. De Boer has not so far found any place where the phrase damping factor is treated synonymously with neper frequency except in undergraduate circuits and linear systems textbooks or World Wide Web pages presenting similar content. This limited context is too narrow to justify introducing the phrase damping factor here.

A nearly similar term, damping ratio, (not "factor") is uniformly well defined in many contexts, including RLC circuits, but the neper frequency (α in our textbook) and the damping ratio are not the same thing. Using our textbook's notation, the damping ratio, ζ, would be defined as ζ =  α/ωo. Since most engineering students will encounter the term damping ratio in their careers, the confusingly similar name, damping factor (also known as damping attenuation factor, attenuatin factor, and damping coefficient, etc.) should not be introduced in the context of RLC circuits. The name neper frequency for α is more memorable, more universal in the literature, and thus more helpful.
Also see related errata on pages 323, 361, and I-3.         (Posted 10/28/2010.)

Page 350. About 2/3 down the page
Change "We must keep in mind that the principle of duality is limited to planar circuits. Nonplanar circuits have no duals, as they cannot be described by a system of mesh equations." to

"We must keep in mind that the method described here for finding a dual is limited to planar circuits. Finding a dual for a nonplanar circuit is beyond the scope of this textbook because nonplanar circuits cannot be described by a system of mesh equations."
Methods for finding a dual for a non-planar circuit can be found in the literature. For one example, A. Bloch, "On Methods for the Construction of Networks Dual to Non-Planar Networks," Proceedings of the Physical Society, Volume 58, Number 6, November 1, 1946.

The basis for duality is found in the duality of electric and magnetic fields implied by the Lorentz transformation. The Lorentz transformation has its roots in the theory of special relativity. Thus the theory of duality found in electric circuits is not restricted to planar circuits, only the method shown in this textbook is so restricted. However the utility of the method shown in this textbook merits its mention.

Pages 534–535 First paragraph in Sec. 12.10.
The second, third, fourth, and fifth sentences of Section 12.10 contain errors. Replace "The wye source connection is used for. . . lighting and appliances use single-phase power." with:

The wye connection is commonly chosen for most sources. This is due to at least three factors. First, there is a neutral connection that can be connected to earth for safety. Second the wye connection gives line voltages that are a factor of the square root of three greater than a delta connection gives. In the case of long distance power transmission, higher line voltages reduce the line currents and consequently reduce the power losses caused by heating of the conductors (I2R). Third, with appropriate voltage levels, single-phase loads can be connected to the three-phase system by connecting them between any single line and neutral. For example in North America 208 VRMS line-to-line three-phase systems will deliver 120 VRMS from line to neutral. Delta connected sources are rarely used because any slight imbalance between the three phases can cause problematic circulating currents, especially when there is no load. (Circulating currents are not a problem in delta-connected loads because each phase is absorbing power.)

*This textbook contains a systematic error.
In many cases where an expression is integrated with respect to time the same variable, t, is used as the variable of integration and as the upper limit of the integral. One variable should not be used for two different quantities. In these cases some other variable such as λ should be substituted for the variable of integration. As an example, the first instance of this error is in equation 6.5 on page 218. Many instances of this problem have not yet been itemized in this errata sheet.

Extensions and Clarifications of text material  (Not errata.)

Page 459. More information on Eq. (11.5).
Equation (11.5) can be further developed to show the reactive power component. (This in addition to the development of the average power component that is already shown in the textbook.) Rearranging the second term in Eq. (11.5) to deliberately itemize the angle (θv – θi), the same as in the first term, gives

p(t) = (1/2)VmIm cos(θv – θi) + (1/2)VmIm cos[2(ωt + θv) – (θv – θi)]

Now apply the trigonometric identity cos(α – β) = cos(α)cos(β) + sin(α)sin(β)
to the second term to get

p(t) = (1/2)VmIm cos(θv – θi) +
          (1/2)VmIm cos[2(ωt + θv)]cos(θv – θi) +
          (1/2)VmIm sin[2(ωt + θv)]sin(θv – θi)

Associate the middle term above with the first term and factor out constant amounts. The instantaneous power then is:

p(t) = (1/2)VmIm cos(θv – θi)[1 + cos(2ωt + θv)] +
          (1/2)VmIm sin(θv – θi)sin[2(ωt + θv)]                                     (11.5b)

The first term above in Eq. (11.5b) represents the average power. The pulsation of the average power is represented via the time-varying multiplicative term,
[1 + cos(2ωt + θv)]. Note that this term varys between 0 and 2 as time varys. It is never negative and has an average value of 1. The coefficient in front of it then must be the average power.

P = (1/2)VmIm cos(θv – θi)

The above equation is identical to Eq. (11.8) on page 460 in the text.

Turning our attention to the second term in Eq. (11.5b) notice that it varys both positive and negative as time varys. This term has an average value of zero. It represents power that is surging back and forth and doing no net work. The second term in Eq. (11.5b) is called the instantaneous reactive power. The peak magnitude of the power that surges back and forth is defined as the amount of reactive power. We use the variable Q to represent reactive power. Reactive power is given the units of "volt-amperes reactive" and is abbreviated as "VAR" or "VARs" (plural). This abbreviation is often pronounced as a word. The peak magnitude of the second term in Eq (11.5b) is

Q = (1/2)VmIm sin(θv – θi)

DEFINITION: Reactive power is the peak magnitude of power that surges equally back and forth. It has units of volt-amperes reactive (VAR).

Now the instantanious power, Eq. (11.5b), can be expressed in terms of the average power, P, and the reactive power, Q.

p(t) = P[1 + cos(2ωt + θv)] + Qsin(2(ωt + θv))                                     (11.5c)

In Section 11.4 of this textbook it is shown that

Vrms = Vm/(sqrt(2)) and that Irms = Im/(sqrt(2))

Applying these equalities gives.

P = VrmsIrms cos(θv – θi) ,      Q = VrmsIrms sin(θv – θi)

The above equations match Eq. (11.50) on page 474 of the text.

This illustrates the calculation of the average power and the reactive power for sinusoidal currents and voltages. The angle (θv – θi) recurrs many times in discussions of AC circuits. For this reason in most textbooks and magzine articles it is simply denoted as θ. This angle is given the name power factor angle.

DEFINITION: The power factor angle is by definition θ = (θv – θi).

Using this definition, Eq. 11.50 on page 474 can be reduced to

P = VrmsIrms cos(θ) ,          Q = VrmsIrms sin(θ)
